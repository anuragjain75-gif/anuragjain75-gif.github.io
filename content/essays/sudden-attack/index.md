The forest has a name that translates as sudden attack.

I did not know this when we set out from Bilaspur in a cavalcade of three cars—my parents, uncles, aunts, and cousins—heading toward what everyone had described, with magnificent vagueness, as some natural place. Nobody had mentioned tigers. A tiger, I suppose, is very much part of nature, and so the others had not thought it necessary to single the animal out as a discrete element in the nature we were going to watch. As is the way with the great Indian family, there had been detailed conversation about the food, the stay arrangements, and the temples on the route.

In my own mind, I had quietly deleted the temples.

Rains had washed the city the day before. The air is squeaky clean as we leave Bilaspur, and the clouds are still hugging the horizon—sparing us the full glare of the sun while assuring us it will not rain today. Familiar scenes tumble past the window: shanty shops, men on khatiyas sipping tea in dhabas, street urchins with matted hair chasing dogs, young girls with brooms. Gradually the city thins; trees take the place of buildings and people. And suddenly, the Maikal mountain range looms ahead, filling the windshield with its untamed beauty.

I sit up—aroused—the way a baby wakes from sleep, eyes glinting in the soft rays of dawn.

As children, we watched Mowgli, Bagheera, Shere Khan, Akela, and Baloo on television—lolling on drawing-room sofas in a world diametrically opposite to the one on screen. Beneath the comfort of my cosmopolitan life, I had shared a primal kinship with Mowgli’s world that I never spoke about to anyone. The Maikal hills, unassuming in height—two hundred to a thousand meters—boasted India’s largest forests: the Satpuda Maikal landscape, the country that inspired Kipling to write The Jungle Book—Mowgli's country. And today I was in it.

Entering the forest, the road immediately changes character. It narrows, serpentines, and negotiates hairpin bends and switchbacks. Straight lines and open spaces of the highways vanish. The sun filters through the canopy in long beams like light falling through the dome windows of a cathedral, except that this cathedral preceded every cathedral ever built and will outlast them all.

Sal, Haldu, Bija, and Mahua trees line both sides—their trunks straight and unhurried—upholding the spirit of the forest, a repository of unfathomable past stories. It was the branch of a Sal tree that Buddha’s mother gripped when she gave birth to him. It was under twin Sal trees that he breathed his last—the trees raining flowers on him as he died.

Rows and rows of them stand now like sentinels of the forest goddess Aranyani—she who is fond of quiet glades, fearless of remote places, and who wanders far from the fringe of civilization without becoming afraid or lonely. She wears anklets with bells. She is rarely seen. She is heard.

Suddenly, a Bollywood song explodes from the car stereo; my uncle has turned on the music. The trees absorb the song without comment. They have absorbed worse.

In my eighth-grade literature textbook, I encountered a passage from The Jungle Book that stopped me:

It was the jackal — Tabaqui, the Dish-licker — and the wolves of India despise Tabaqui because he runs about making mischief, and telling tales… But they are afraid of him too, because Tabaqui, more than anyone else in the jungle, is apt to go mad, and then he forgets that he was ever afraid of anyone, and runs through the forest biting everything in his way. Even the tiger runs and hides when little Tabaqui goes mad, for madness is the most disgraceful thing that can overtake a wild creature.

I read this and recognized myself immediately. Not Mowgli — the free child raised by wolves, beloved of the jungle, moving between worlds with grace. Not Bagheera — elegant, dark, controlled. Not even Shere Khan, the tiger of real power. I was Tabaqui. The small, despised one who goes suddenly and inexplicably mad. The one whose anger is disproportionate, who bites everything in his way when he gets angry.

For a long stretch of my life, this was precisely true. The madness was real — a volatile, restless energy that could find no container in the world being offered to me. Schools did not know what to do with it. Careers offered it a cage called ambition. Family life gentled it somewhat, the way water smooths a stone—not by changing its nature but by wearing down its sharpest edges.

What actually tamed the jackal was not discipline. What tamed it was the realization that the madness was a distorted form of wildness. Tabaqui goes mad because he is a wild creature forced to live on the scraps of more powerful animals, running their errands, eating their leavings, and never inhabiting his own nature fully. The madness is what happens to wildness when it cannot find its proper home in the true wild.

Here, in Mowgli’s country, something in my chest is very quiet. I breathe deeply, mildly intoxicated and lulled by the forest silence: no more living on scraps.

The music stops. The car slows.

We have reached a small bridge over the Maniyari River. Everyone climbs out for photographs. I spring from the car for an entirely different reason.

The fragrance of Sal flowers hits me first—a clean, faintly sweet scent, carried on the breeze like a rumor of something larger. Then I see the water.

The Maniyari winds through dense forest and hills before reaching this point, and because of the black soil of the Maikal range, it runs the color of dark chocolate—not the silver or turquoise of mountain rivers, not the color of what we imagine when we hear the word "river." My cousin tells me the name "Maniyari" means "a garland of pearls"—the river in its sinuous path through the hills appearing like pearls strung together.

I look at the chocolate-brown water.

Not exactly pearls. But yes, it shines like one.

I stand on the bridge and watch the eddies and whirls below. The stream is dry in patches, the sand bone-white in the filtered light. Green branches droop over the water and almost kiss it. I want to leave the bridge and follow the river into the forest until the trees close behind me.

Behind me, my parents and aunts and uncles arrange themselves for a photograph. They call out. I turn, oblige, and turn back to complete my conversation with the garland of pearls.

Everyone is filing back into the car. We have to cross the bridge, under which the river flows. The river is still there. It was not waiting for me. It did not need to wait. It has been here since before the Maikal Hills had a name, and I render a silent prayer that it shall still be there, garlanding its lover—the Maikal Hills—long after my bones are interred in the earth.

We get back into the car to resume our journey. As we enter deeper, the forest floor catches fire.

Cannas. An unbelievably long stretch of them—blood-red, blazing, setting the leaf litter alight with color. My mother has always planted Cannas at our gate. My entire childhood is wound through their colors: blood-red, orange, pale yellow, apricot, salmon, and the luscious green of their leaves in the morning with dewdrops resting on them like small, temporary pearls.

Until the eighth grade, my relationship with nature was like Adam’s in the garden. Then the board exams arrived. The fruits of knowledge were consumed. The expulsion was swift and total.

It took twenty years after college to find the way back.

Twenty years of slowly recognizing what had been lost—and then, through slow toil, refusing to continue losing it: leaving my corporate job, starting self-inquiry, educating underprivileged children, doing woodworking with recycled timber, and tending a garden by hand. Patiently, weathering emotional and financial storms, I built a life organized around silence rather than productivity. I crafted a frugal enough existence that early retirement became possible, and with it, the freedom that feels like a leaf releasing from a branch.

We arrive at the gateway to Achanakmar Tiger Reserve.

It is more beautiful than I expected—carved bison on the pillars, deer on the capitals, and a reclining tiger embossed above the words of welcome. The gateway honors both the killer and the killed. The tiger that gave this place its name attacked without warning, without negotiation, without the long preamble of civilization. It simply acted from its nature.

I stand looking at the reclining tiger above the gate.

Sudden attack.

The words remind me not only of the tiger’s sudden attack on the British officer that named this forest but also of the forest’s sudden attack on everything the city has built inside me. The Sal trees, the Aranyani silence, the chocolate river, the Cannas—dismantling things quietly, the way a forest dismantles a wall—not with force but with patience, with the slow insistence of roots finding their way through concrete.

I am not Tabaqui anymore. I am not sure I am Mowgli either.

I am a man standing at a gate in a forest, feeling—without anxiety, without need for explanation—that this is where he belongs.

My cousin touches my shoulder. The family has moved inside. There is a guest house—modern but tastefully built, with olive green walls painted with white tribal motifs and bamboo furniture glowing golden in the dappled light. A yellow-footed green pigeon struts in the grass, attending to its own business with the dignified indifference of a creature that has never doubted its right to be here.

I find a bench under a tree and sit, watching the forest creeping in all directions, filled with the silent satisfaction of having earned my right to be here in its sanctuary.

The jackal has come home.

The Achanakmar-Amarkantak Biosphere Reserve is a UNESCO-recognized protected area in Chhattisgarh, India. It is home to 26 tigers, significant populations of leopards, wild dogs, and sloth bears and is a critical watershed of peninsular India. It is one of the least-visited and least-photographed tiger reserves in the country — which is, perhaps, exactly why the forest goddess still lives there.
