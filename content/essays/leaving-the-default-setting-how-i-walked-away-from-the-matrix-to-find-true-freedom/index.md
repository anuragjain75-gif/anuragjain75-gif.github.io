I wake up in the morning and toddle towards my parents’ bedroom when suddenly I see golden sunlight bursting through the window, bathing the room with an ethereal light.

I have spent almost my whole life in this town called Tatanagar, the Pittsburgh of India. Built from scratch in the early 20th century as a model “company town,” it was engineered to combine heavy steel production with spacious public parks and tree-lined avenues. Growing up here meant that in my consciousness the images of smokestacks, furnaces, and heavy industry mingled with the golden glow of sunlight in well-planned homes; the fragrance of globe-shaped orange-yellow flowers of the Kadam trees along the roadside; and the spectacular vision of the Dalma Mountains looming on the horizon.

This was the consciousness that accompanied me when I was bundled off to “Little Flower School” as a little flower myself. In an era before school vans, my younger brother and I skipped and hopped our way home, sometimes getting soaking wet in the monsoon rains. We would rip off our shoes and socks to sail paper boats down the gushing roadside gutters, splashing in cratered puddles and shouting with glee—entirely oblivious to the dressing-down our soiled clothes would earn us later.

On one such carefree afternoon in upper kindergarten, something shifted. I arrived home to find my mother opening the door with a grim, heavy face.

“You have performed miserably,” she said. “Your rank is 35th in the class.”

It might have been 30th, 36th, or 37th; the exact digit has blurred. What remains perfectly preserved is my utter perplexity. How could the 35th position be bad? I wondered. In a child’s universe, a bigger number meant more of something. More was better. Higher was winning.

Was it not?

It did not take long for me to decode the correct algorithm for winning. A potent combination of temperament, family conditioning, and the competitive atmosphere around me made winning into a habit. But not entirely. For even as I ratcheted up the ranks through school, at the core, I remained a romantic. In the stillness of the biology class, as my teacher droned on about the structure of the plant cell, my eyes would stray over to the glass window panes where drops of falling rain made mysterious, trickling patterns. I slowly developed a dual consciousness—paying attention to the teacher’s words while another part of me disappeared into the silent contemplation of nature. It was a habit that would save me in college, where my disconnect with formal academics reached its zenith.

In grade five, a seemingly casual word from my mother became the greatest force in my life—shaping my entire being and continuing to do so nearly half a century later. I am certain she did not intend the monumentality of what she unleashed. Her goal was far more banal when she remarked one day that intelligent people do not just read school textbooks but other books as well. Little did she know she had just opened up galaxies of knowledge. By the time I eventually encountered Advaita’s mahavakya, “You are Brahman,” the thousands of books I had devoured over the years had already prepared the ground for that recognition. The habit of reading became my cornerstone—not only for successfully navigating the world but also for liberating myself from it. In the many quirks of irony that were to play out in my life, this was perhaps the greatest: the very mother who introduced a metric of performance in my early years (Rank 35) unintentionally handed me the very tool—books and reading—that would ultimately allow me to dissolve the performance trap entirely.

At the center of the knowledge revolution of my school days was the library of Telco Club. I grew up in an era untouched by mobiles, cable TV, or the internet. In this cloister of pre-digital silence, the library became my temple. I initiated myself into its sanctuary with The Hardy Boys—mystery novels for young readers revolving around teenage amateur sleuths solving cases that often stumped their adult counterparts. I don’t remember a single plot today, but their spirit of adventure seeped into me. Instantly, the walls of my consciousness—constructed by the rigid blueprint of life in the town of Telco, in the country of India, in the 1980s—started crumbling due to a collision with a totally different culture. Sherlock Holmes, Agatha Christie, and Arthur Hailey followed swiftly, each opening a portal into a different world, steadily dismantling the boundaries of the environment I was born into.

The fire stoked by those early mysteries gradually became an inferno, devouring books across genres—biographies, science fiction, psychology, history, and eventually, philosophy. Something within this vast, quiet consumption must have radiated outward, because in the eighth grade, my classmates elected me the ‘wisest person in the class’—even though I was not the class topper. It strikes me today that even at that tender age, children could intuitively distinguish between academic brilliance and a deeper wisdom, however nominal. Has it got something to do with the pre-digital silence of that era?

Looking back, I call the eighth grade the golden year of my school life. I was thirteen, and not only had I fallen in love with knowledge, but I also discovered the scintillating power of analytical thinking through a newfound passion for mathematics—specifically, geometry.

Being an innate romantic, I had never been drawn to numbers, and my grades usually reflected it. I had only managed to maintain a high class rank up until the seventh grade because of my strength in the humanities and sciences, which had not yet incorporated numerical skills.

The turning point came on an ordinary day at a local shop. I was with my younger brother, who, up to then, routinely scored lower ranks than me but possessed an exceptional mind for math. When I miscalculated our bill and handed over too much money, my brother instantly chided me for my poor math skills. The sting of that moment ran deep. Right then and there, I vowed to better myself.

My father, a true lover of mathematics, delighted in stepping in to guide my studies. However, his method was unique: he would insist on solving the exact same problem through three or four entirely different approaches. At the time, I found this exercise exasperating and downright irritating.

Yet, later in life, that very irritation became a cornerstone of my consciousness. It trained my mind to view the same reality from multiple perspectives. The attitude my father brought to mathematics gradually spilled beyond textbooks into my work, my relationships, and ultimately, my philosophy.

But the legacy that mathematics bequeathed to me went even deeper. My eighth-grade math teacher, fondly remembered to this day, once told us that Abraham Lincoln had taught himself geometry in adulthood. While serving in Congress, Lincoln immersed himself in Euclid’s Six Books in order to discipline his mind in deductive reasoning. That spark lit the hidden gunpowder in my mind. Through geometry, I unlocked a new dimension of consciousness: the power to sustain deep and abstract thought.

Back in the 1980s, India was flooded with high-quality Soviet literature sold at astonishingly affordable prices. At local book fairs, I began hunting for Russian geometry volumes filled with labyrinthine problems far beyond anything required by our school syllabus. I was no longer solving mathematics merely to score marks on an exam—those came anyway as a natural by-product. What I had inadvertently stumbled upon was a higher form of intellectual pleasure: abstract contemplation.

For days, I would wrestle with a single geometry problem, holding my mind steadily upon it until the solution suddenly revealed itself in a flash of insight. Years later, in the midst of self-inquiry, I discovered that the same disciplined patience could be turned toward the far more difficult psychological questions of human life.

By the end of eighth grade, the lights illuminating the salad days of my rich interior life began to dim. I was slowly absorbed into the matrix of board exams and competitive entrance tests designed to secure admission into a good engineering college. The freedom to shape my own inner world gradually slipped away as I was pulled onto the conveyor belt of an industrial education system.

The kind of deep, sustained thinking that geometry had awakened in me suddenly became a liability. The system rewarded speed, efficiency, and specialization, leaving little room for the wide-ranging curiosity nurtured by years of reading and contemplation.

The sunlight may still have streamed ethereally through my bedroom windows. The rains may still have traced mysterious patterns upon them. The fragrance of kadam flowers may still have drifted through the evening air—I honestly do not know. All that remains in my memory from those years is the relentless pressure of examinations.

Yet a small residue of my earlier consciousness still remained alive within me. At a time when nearly every classmate enrolled in coaching institutes to prepare for the engineering entrance examinations, I chose to study alone, instinctively recoiling from the mechanical grind of that culture. I had not yet developed the strength to break free from the matrix entirely, but this quiet act of resistance gave me a little breathing space within it.

I did not gain admission into India’s most elite engineering institutions. But I managed to find my way into a college of considerable national standing—and perhaps, more importantly, I managed to preserve a fragile inner world that had not yet been completely swallowed by the system.
